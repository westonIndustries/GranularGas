If you are looking at these specific datasets (likely from a state agency like Washington's OFM or a regional council of governments), they are structured as time-series snapshots. They don't just show a total; they show how a specific geography evolved every single year on April 1st.

Here is a breakdown of what you will find inside each CSV:
1. April 1 Population Estimates (CSV)

This file tracks people. It is the baseline used for allocating tax revenue and determining infrastructure needs.

    Geographic Identifiers: Rows are organized by Federal Information Processing Series (FIPS) codes, County names, and City/Town names.

    The "Census Base": Every 10 years (1990, 2000, 2010, 2020), there is a "Correction" or "Base" column. This is the actual physical count conducted by the Census Bureau.

    Intercensal & Postcensal Estimates: The years between the Decennial Census (e.g., 2011–2019) are estimates. These are calculated using "symptomatic data" like birth/death certificates, driver's license changes, and school enrollments.

    Total Population: The primary number.

    Group Quarters: A specific column for people not living in traditional households (e.g., military barracks, college dorms, correctional facilities). This is crucial for models because these populations don't drive "housing demand" in the traditional sense.

2. April 1 Housing Unit Estimates (CSV)

This file tracks structures. It tells you the physical capacity of a region to hold its population.

    Total Housing Units: The total count of all residential structures, whether occupied or vacant.

    Unit Type Breakdown: This is the most valuable part of the data. It typically categorizes units into:

        One-Unit (Single Family): Detached homes.

        Two or More Units (Multi-Family): Duplexes, townhomes, and large apartment complexes.

        Mobile Homes/Special: Manufactured housing.

    Occupancy & Household Size: Often, these files include an estimate of Persons Per Household (PPH). This is a critical variable: if the population grows but PPH shrinks (e.g., more people living alone), you need more houses even if the population stays the same.

Why "April 1"?

You might wonder why it isn't "January 1." In the U.S., April 1st is the official "Census Day." By releasing estimates for April 1st every year, demographers can maintain a perfect "apples-to-apples" comparison with the official Decennial Census data without having to adjust for seasonal migration or three months of "drift."
Common Use Case

If you merge these two, you create a Housing Gap Analysis. By taking the Population and dividing it by the Total Housing Units, you can see the Vacancy Rate trends. A declining ratio over 30 years usually indicates a housing shortage and rising prices.
