In NW Natural’s 2025 Integrated Resource Plan (IRP), the company has updated its Use Per Customer (UPC) forecasts to reflect significant shifts in the Pacific Northwest’s energy landscape.

The most critical figure from the 2025 IRP (specifically from Chapter 4 and Technical Working Group 9) is the system-wide annual load decline, which is the aggregate result of falling UPC across the residential and commercial sectors.

Why the Forecast has "Steepened" since 2022

NW Natural identifies three primary reasons why customers are using less gas today than they were just three years ago:

    Climate Warming Trends: The 2025 IRP modeling now explicitly accounts for a declining trend in Heating Degree Days (HDDs). Winters are getting milder, reducing the core "space heating" load that drives the majority of UPC.

    Building Codes (WA & OR): In Washington, the 2023 codes requiring heat pump technology have effectively "zeroed out" new gas load for certain segments. In Oregon, the Reach Code and increasing efficiency standards mean a new home's UPC is roughly 30-40% lower than a 1990s vintage home.

    The "Hybrid Heating" Shift: The IRP now favors a Hybrid Heating scenario (electric heat pump with gas backup) as a primary decarbonization tool. This drastically reduces UPC because gas is only used during "peak" events (sub-35°F), rather than for the entire heating season.

Modeling Recommendation

To align your RBSA data with NW Natural’s expectations:

    In Python/R: Apply a decay factor of 0.988 (representing the -1.19% net decline) to your annual consumption variables for each year of your forecast.

    Peak vs. Annual: Note that while annual UPC is declining, Peak Day requirements remain robust. NW Natural models the residential peak hour usage at roughly 0.092% of their total annual weather-normalized load.
