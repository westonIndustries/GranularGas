To find defensible Baseload Consumption Factors (cooking, drying, and fireplaces), you should triangulate between the Pacific Northwest-specific RBSA sub-metering data and the DOE/EIA RECS 2020 (released in late 2023).

Baseload is often defined in gas modeling as the consumption that remains constant in the summer months (non-heating season), though fireplaces are a "hybrid" load that can skew these numbers if not properly isolated.

2. Using RBSA Sub-Metered Data (The "Deep Dive")

If you have the RBSA metering zip files (specifically the RBSAM or End-Use Load Research datasets), you can extract more granular "real-world" factors.

    Extraction Method: Filter the EndUse column for Cooking, Dryer, and Other (which often captures fireplaces).

    The "Summer Baseline" Technique: If you are using billing data without sub-metering, look at July/August usage. Subtract the estimated Water Heating load (approx. 15–20 therms/month) to isolate the residual cooking/drying baseload.

    Fireplace "Clustering": RBSA data shows that gas fireplaces in the PNW are often used in "bursts" during shoulder seasons (Oct/Nov and Mar/Apr). If your model is monthly, don't spread the fireplace load evenly; weight it 70% toward the winter months.

3. DOE/EIA RECS 2020 Reference Values

For a "national defensibility" check, the EIA RECS 2020 dataset (Table CE4.1) provides the most recent federal end-use estimates.

    Cooking: 2.8 million BTUs (~28 therms) per year.

    Clothes Drying: 2.2 million BTUs (~22 therms) per year.

    Fireplaces: RECS groups this under "Other," but for Pacific Northwest-specific households, it averages roughly 55 therms/year when a gas fireplace is present.

For your model to be truly precise, you need to account for the "phantom" load of standing pilot lights. In older Pacific Northwest housing stock (pre-2010 vintages in the RBSA), these small, continuous flames can represent a surprisingly large percentage of the "baseload" consumption.

Modern codes (like the 2024/2026 Washington State Energy Code) effectively ban these in new equipment, but they remain highly prevalent in the existing PNW stock, especially in fireplaces and older water heaters.

2. The "Useful Heat" Discount

Not all pilot light energy is "wasted." In the cold PNW climate, pilot energy often contributes to the building's thermal envelope:

    Water Heaters: Approximately 75% of the pilot energy actually helps maintain tank temperature, reducing the frequency of the main burner cycles.

    Fireplaces: Most heat escapes through the flue (especially in "B-vent" or masonry chimneys). Assume only 10–15% effectiveness as room heat.

    Cooking: Primarily wasted/added to cooling load in summer.

3. Modeling the "Ignition Transition" by Vintage

You can use the RBSA Vintage field to determine the probability of a standing pilot vs. an Intermittent Pilot Ignition (IPI) or Hot Surface Ignition (HSI) system:

    Pre-1990 Stock: 90% probability of standing pilots for water heaters and furnaces.

    1990–2014 Stock: High probability of electronic ignition for furnaces, but 70% probability of standing pilots for water heaters and fireplaces (Millivolt systems).

    2015–2026 Stock: <5% probability of standing pilots. Federal standards and regional codes (WA/OR) have effectively phased them out for everything except specific "off-grid" or specialty appliances.

Strategic Recommendation: The "Summer Kill" Switch

In the PNW, a common homeowner behavior (often cited in NW Natural and ETO studies) is turning off the fireplace pilot in May and relighting it in October.

    Model Assumption: If you are modeling a "high-efficiency" persona, reduce the fireplace pilot load by 50% to account for this seasonal behavior.

One Final Modeling Note

In the NEEA RBSA Metering Study (Ecotope, 2014/2022), researchers found that the actual "in-situ" standby loss for gas water heaters was often 20% higher than the lab-rated Energy Factor (EF) suggested. This is due to "thermosiphoning"—heat escaping through the copper pipes connected to the top of the tank. If you want to be extremely precise, add a 1.2x "Plumbing Penalty" to the standby values for any site that doesn't have pipe insulation listed in its RBSA audit.
