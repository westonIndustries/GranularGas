Infiltration & Air Leakage Modeling: PNW Residential Stock

The air leakage parameters in this model are derived from the 2022 RBSA Final Report and Blower Door (CFM50) testing data collected across the Northwest.

Key Methodological Steps:

    Standardized Leakage (ACH50​): All field-measured leakage is converted to Air Changes per Hour at 50 Pascals (ACH50​) by normalizing the measured cubic feet per minute (CFM50) against the home's conditioned volume.

    LBL Infiltration Credit (N-Factor): To convert high-pressure blower door results to "natural" daily infiltration (ACHnat​), the model applies the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBL) N-factor. A regional default of N=20 is used for the Pacific Northwest, though this factor is adjusted based on building height and local shielding (urban vs. rural).

    Stack & Wind Effects: The infiltration UA accounts for the "Stack Effect" (buoyancy of warm air) and wind-driven pressure. Older RBSA vintages (pre-1980) typically exhibit ACH50​ values of 10.0–15.0, whereas new construction under modern WA/OR codes is required to be ≤ 3.0.

    Thermal Impact: Air leakage is modeled as a sensible heat loss using the specific heat of air (approx. 0.018Btu/ft3⋅°F at sea level).

Heat Pump Retrofit Methodology: PNW Residential Stock

The Heat Pump scenario in this model accounts for the shifting efficiency (HSPF2) and thermal requirements of the Pacific Northwest climate.

Key Methodological Steps:

    Thermal Balance Point: The model calculates the "Balance Point"—the outdoor temperature at which the home's heat loss (UA) exactly matches the heat pump's maximum output. In Portland (Climate Zone 4), a "Heat Pump Ready" home typically targets a balance point below 30°F.

    Weatherization Logic: In the Heat_Pump scenario, the model assumes a "Retrofit Package" is applied to older vintages (Pre-1980) to bring their Infiltration (ACH50​) and Wall U-values closer to 1990s standards. This prevents excessive "strip heat" usage during Gorge wind events.

    HSPF2 Scaling: Efficiencies are modeled using the newer HSPF2 (Heating Seasonal Performance Factor) standard, which accounts for higher external static pressure in ductwork, a common issue identified in the 2022 RBSA.
