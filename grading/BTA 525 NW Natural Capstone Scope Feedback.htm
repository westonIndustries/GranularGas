1 Background 

Scoping document articulates sufficient client background and description of the problem. 

 

Score: 5/5

 

Instructor Feedback: The background section is excellent. It clearly explains NW Natural’s regulatory context, the purpose of the IRP, and the limitations of the current top-down forecasting approach. The team does a strong job connecting technical forecasting methodology to strategic uncertainty (electrification, efficiency, policy shifts), which elevates the problem from a modeling exercise to a planning capability question. The narrative demonstrates solid domain understanding and frames the opportunity in a way that feels relevant and consequential.

 

Client questions/feedback: The statement “IRP demand forecasting approach relies primarily on top-down methodologies” is not entirely true. Our UPC model is a bottom-up forecast. We have also moved towards bottom-up forecasting for our C&I customers with a recent methodology update.

2 Project Objective

Clearly state the objective of the project. The “north star.”

 

Score: 5/5

 

Instructor Feedback: The objective is concise, focused, and aligned with the problem statement. It clearly communicates that the goal is to design and evaluate a bottom-up framework that complements existing forecasting methods. The emphasis on long-term scenario insight under evolving conditions is especially strong. The “north star” is well defined and appropriately scoped for a capstone project.

 

Client questions/feedback: We want to focus on residential end use for this Capstone. Commercial and industrial are harder to predict/understand as customers are more heterogeneous.

 

3 In Scope / Out of Scope

Clearly put bounds, at a high level, of what the team will do, and will not do.

 

Score: 5/5

 

Instructor Feedback: The in-scope and out-of-scope sections are well articulated and appropriately bounded. The exclusion of production-level deployment, regulatory filing use, and operational integration is especially important and well stated. The sponsor requested additional clarity which is a good iterative process to go through with a client.

 

Client questions/feedback: The client requested clarification around model maturity and scope specificity, including: “Does illustrative mean workable? What is the difference between illustrative and production-level?” “Can you please provide more information about what your thinking is here?” “I would like some subtasks here within the model design. It will be useful to understand what the outputs will be, what the envisaged structure of the model will be, and what the anticipated data sources and inputs will be before seeing a fully baked model, even if it is envisaged as an MVP.”

4 Work Plan

The workplan is structured in overlapping phases with tasks defined in each phase.  For each task, clarity on the activity description (what it is), planned timeline (start and finish), who owns it, and any associated deliverables (when appropriate)

 

Score: 4/5

 

Instructor Feedback: The phased structure (initiation, data exploration, model design, validation, synthesis) is logical and professional. The sequencing reflects a disciplined approach and is appropriate for a four-month capstone. The team will add granularity to the schedule as the project gets started and the team gets familiar with the data set, the available outputs and the effort required to create the deliverable.

 

Client questions/feedback: The client requested additional clarity and additional details (see comment above)

 

5 Deliverables

There should be a clear description of the final deliverables.  Final deliverables must include written work as a requirement of the PSU graduation, and a management presentation.  But other deliverables and interim deliverables should be defined and may include a research summary at mid-project, or spreadsheet tools and/or videos training the client.

 

Score: 4/5

 

Instructor Feedback: Deliverables are clearly defined and aligned with both PSU graduation requirements and client value: methodology documentation, comparative analysis, recommendations, a final report, and a management-level presentation. The team appropriately distinguishes analytical insight from operational deployment. The sponsor’s comments suggest an opportunity to clarify the indicative structure and outputs of the model, but the deliverable set itself is comprehensive and well framed.

 

Client questions/feedback: The client asked for additional clarity regarding model outputs and structure, noting: A desire to see “indicative outputs, indicative structure, and data links” associated with the proposed end-use simulation model. Clarification regarding what the model will concretely produce before it is built.

6 Research Plan

The research plan deeply describes the expected research activities. This includes specific tasks associated with each phase (can be less detailed in the later phases if the team updates the plan).  A description of secondary and primary research is included.  With respect to primary research, internal and external interviews are specified, with a description of the interview target categories (often the specific people are not yet known, especially external interviewees), and the target number of completed interviews for each category. Some projects will use surveys. For each interview or survey, the research plan should include what is hoped to be learned, and some initial questions.

 

Score: 5/5

 

Instructor Feedback: The research plan reflects strong methodological thinking. The combination of secondary research (industry best practices in bottom-up forecasting) and primary research (structured SME interviews) is appropriate and well aligned with the project’s strategic nature.

 

Client questions/feedback: The client emphasized: “We want to focus on residential end use for this Capstone. Commercial and industrial are harder to predict/understand as customers are more heterogeneous.” “Research on appliance performance will also be useful.”

7 Accountability & Organization

Roles for each teammate should be clear.  This will include a project coordinator and research coordinator, and additional division of responsibilities among the tasks grouped into a logical organization. The meeting and communication cadence should also be clear and should be scheduled for the duration of the project. Weekly team meetings, regular meetings with the Faculty Advisor and client should be defined.

 

Score: 5/5

 

Instructor Feedback: Roles are clearly defined and professionally structured, with designated project and research coordinators. The weekly meeting cadence and structured stakeholder engagement plan demonstrate strong execution readiness. The team composition reflects meaningful domain expertise in energy and analytics, which is well aligned with the project. This section inspires confidence in delivery capability over a four-month engagement.

 

Client questions/feedback:  The client noted an additional stakeholder to include: “Taylor Toews is also part of the team. She is the company’s lead PLEXOS modeler and gave the technical deep dive presentation.”
